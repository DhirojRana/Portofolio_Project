Objectives

  2. Contrast and apply several methods for interacting with a system.

  6. Design human-computer dialogues, including the use of dialogue diagramming.
  7. Design graphical user interfaces.


Chapter Overview

The purpose of this chapter is to introduce students to the process of designing interfaces and dialogues. The content of this chapter naturally extends the topics presented in Chapter 14, which means that the guidelines for designing forms and reports also apply to the design of human-computer interfaces. Additionally, form and report design and interface and dialogue design have similar processes and deliverables. Thus, your lectures and exercises should stress this similarity, to emphasize the importance of the right information, in the right amount of detail, at the right time. Some instructors prefer to teach these topics together to avoid redundancy and to show the linkages between the chapters. We have found that teaching these topics either together or separately to be equally effective.
The chapter first provides a review of human-computer interaction methods and devices. Depending on your overall curriculum, these topics may be somewhat redundant or too rudimentary for your students. If so, simply have the students "skim" this material. The most important parts of this section (and possibly the most interesting to discuss), are the menu design guidelines and Tables 14-3 and 14-4, which contrast interaction devices. Both topics are worthy of some class time. The remainder of the chapter describes the fundamental building blocks and the mechanics for designing interfaces and dialogues.

Introduction.
Interface design focuses on how information is provided to and captured from users; Dialogue design focuses on the sequencing of interface displays. Dialogues are analogous to a conversation between two people. The grammatical rules followed by each person during a conversation are analogus to the interface.

Thus, the design of interfaces and dialogues is the process of of defining the manner in which humans and computers exchange information.

A good human-computer interface provides a uniform structure for finding, viewing, and invoking the different components of a system.

  • Designing Interfaces and Dialogues in Graphical Environments.
    Graphical User Interface (GUI) environment are rapidly growing in popularity. Although all the inteface and dialogue guidelines previously apply to designing GUIs, there are additional issues that are unique to these environments that must be considered. Here, we brievely discuss some of these issues:
    • Graphical Interface Design Issues.
      When designing GUIs for an operating environment such as Microsoft Windows or for the Macintosh, there are numerous factors to consider. Some factors are common to all GUI environments while others are specific to a single environment. Our discussion will focus on a few general truths that experinced designers mention as critical to the design of usable GUIs. In most discussions of GUI programming , two rules repeatedly emerge as comprising the first step to becoming an effective GUI designer:
      1. Become an expert user of the GUI environment.
      2. Understand the available resources and how they can be used.

      Figure 15-21 Highlighting Graphical User Interface Design Standards
    • Dialogue Design Issues in Graphical Environment.
      When designing a dialogue, your goal is to establish the sequence of displays (full screens or windows) that users will encounter when working with the system. Within many GUI environment, this process can be a bit more challenging due to the GUI's ability to suspend activities (without resolving a request for informationor exiting the application all together) and switch to another application or task. For example, within Microsoft Word, the spell checker executes independently from the general word processing. This means that you can easily jump between spell checker and word processor without exiting either one. Conversely, when selecting the print operation, you must either initiate printing or abort this request before returning to the word processing.
      Table 15-14 Common properties of Windows & forms in GUI Environment that can be Active/Inactive
      Property Explanation
      Modality Require users to resolve the request for information before proceeding (e.g., need to cancel or save befor closing a window).
      Resizable Allows users to resize a window or a form (e.g., to make room to see other windows also on the screen).
      Movable Allows users to expand a window or a form (e.g., to allow other windows to be seen).
      Maximize Allows user to expand a window or a form to a full screen in size (e.g., avoid distraction from other active windows or forms
      Minimize Allows user to shrink a window or a form to an icon (e.g., to get the window out of the way while working on other active windos
      System Menu Allows a window or a form to also have a system menu to directly access system-level functions (e.g., to save or copy data).
      One easy way to deal with the complexity of designing advanced graphical user interfaces is to require users to always resolve all requests for information before processing. For such design, the dialogue diagramming technique is adaquate design tool. This, however, would make the system operate in a manner similar to a traditional non-GUI environmentwhere the sequencing of displays is tightly controlled. The drawback to such aproach would be the failure to capitalize on the task-switching capabilities of these environments. Thus, if you want to allow users to selectively perform some functions or selectivily switch between tasks, or more powerful design tool may be needed. One tool is often applied to this problem is the state-transition diagram (STD). STDs can be used to model dialogues where displays are selectively suspended and activated by the user.

      In summary, designing dialogues in environments where the sequence between displays cannot be predetermined offers significant challenges to the designer. In fact, the focus of object-oriented systems analysis and design, a much more advanced design approch, is to provide designers with tools and techniques for modeling systems employing these concepts.
